📖 Help & Guide

New to anti-money-laundering (AML)? Start here. No finance background needed.

What is money laundering?

Criminals earn “dirty” money (from fraud, drugs, scams) and need to make it look like it came from a legitimate source. They do this by moving it through many bank accounts in confusing ways so investigators lose the trail. Argus is the system that spots those suspicious money movements.

How does Argus decide what’s suspicious?

Every money transfer between two accounts is a transaction. Argus draws all of them as a network (a graph): each account is a dot (node) and each transfer is an arrow (edge). An AI model (a Graph Neural Network) gives every transfer a laundering-risk score. The highest-risk transfers get grouped into an alert you can investigate.

We only raise an alert when at least 3 accounts are involved — a single A→B payment is just a payment; laundering needs a chain.

The laundering shapes (patterns)

  • Fan-Out — one account sprays money to many (splitting cash up to hide it).
  • Fan-In — many accounts funnel money into one (collecting it back together).
  • Cycle — money travels in a loop back to where it started (no real business reason).
  • Scatter-Gather / Gather-Scatter — money fans out through middlemen then regroups (adding layers).
  • Bipartite / Complex — denser webs of transfers between groups.

Reading an alert — the red flags

Topology alone isn’t proof (real businesses also fan money in and out). So each alert lists the specific behaviours that separate laundering from normal trade: structuring (many near-identical amounts to stay under reporting limits), pass-through “mules” (an account that forwards ~100% of what it receives), velocity (money racing through many hands in hours), cross-currency hops, and off-hours timing. It’s the combination of signals that matters.

Using the app, tab by tab

  • Dashboard — the big picture: total alerts, money flagged, pattern mix, most-involved banks, and Top Risky Accounts (click one to investigate it).
  • Investigate — pick an alert from the left list (filter by date range), see its network graph, step through each transfer, read the evidence, and click Confirm / Review / Dismiss. Click any node for that account's panel and risk score; right-click for its full transaction graph.
  • Search — look up any account that appears in a flagged transaction and see its 2-hop network: direct counterparties, and theirs in turn.
  • Case Manager — every decision you've made, as an audit trail. Export it all as CSV.
  • Whitelist — mark trusted accounts (e.g. payroll) so they stop generating alerts.
  • Predict — upload your own CSV/Excel of transactions and have the model score them on demand.

How banks fight this today

Traditional systems use fixed rules (“flag anything over $10,000”) and file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs). Criminals dodge fixed thresholds easily. Argus adds an AI layer that learns laundering behaviour across the whole network, so it catches patterns rules miss — while still letting a human analyst make the final call.
